The "Exploration Strategy" guides me to target under-explored regions and parameters. The current population clusters around certain sweep angles, chord lengths, and predominantly negative twists/dihedral. My new design will push boundaries in multiple parameters simultaneously to uncover novel optima.

*   **Explore lower sweep angles**: Most high-performing designs clustered around 63-66 degrees of sweep. To explore an under-represented region, I will choose a significantly lower sweep angle (50.0 degrees) to investigate its impact on vortex formation and induced drag. This will test if a less swept wing can achieve high CL/CDi by relying less on vortex lift and more on efficient spanwise loading.
*   **Test a smaller root chord**: The current successful designs have root chords mostly in the 30-40 inch range. I will explore a much smaller root chord (20.0 inches) to understand the effects of scaling and potentially higher effective aspect ratio on aerodynamic efficiency. This challenges the assumption that larger wings are always better.
*   **Investigate positive dihedral**: All high-performing designs show zero or negative dihedral. Positive dihedral is largely unexplored for delta wings in this context. While generally for lateral stability, its specific aerodynamic impact on CL/CDi in a delta wing environment is unknown. I will choose a moderate positive dihedral (10.0 degrees) to diversify the search.
*   **Try wash-in (positive twist_root, negative twist_tip)**: All previous designs used washout (negative twist at both root and tip, or more negative at tip than root). I will explore a "wash-in" configuration by setting a positive `twist_root` (5.0 degrees) and a negative `twist_tip` (-5.0 degrees). This creates a substantial twist differential where the wing tip is twisted *down* relative to the root, a completely novel load distribution strategy for this population.
*   **Explore extreme thickness with moderate camber**: Most designs are in the 11-18% thickness range. I will push towards the upper limit of thickness (24%) with a moderate camber (NACA 24xx). This combination is unexplored and could reveal unexpected benefits, perhaps in structural integration or high-lift performance, challenging the assumption that thinner is always better for efficiency.
