*   **Novel Sweep Exploration**: The highest reward design (D1) had a sweep of ~63.8 degrees, which is significantly lower than most other designs in the top ranks (e.g., D3 at ~73.4 deg). This suggests that excessive sweep might increase induced drag more than it enhances vortex lift efficiency. I will explore a sweep angle slightly lower than D1's but still within the high-sweep category to investigate this optimal balance further, aiming for a sweet spot for vortex stability and induced drag.
*   **Aggressive Root Washout**: Both Design 1 and Design 3 show strong negative twist at the root (approx. -8.7 to -9.9 degrees). This is a consistent trend for high performance, likely aiding in load distribution towards the inner wing and potentially reducing root stall or improving vortex initiation. I will maintain an aggressive negative twist at the root.
*   **Moderate Tip Washout for Load Distribution**: Design 3, with its high sweep, had a relatively milder tip washout (-4.5 deg) compared to its root washout. This creates a larger twist differential across the span. This strategy could improve lift distribution and delay tip stall, which is critical for delta wings. I will explore a design with a substantial washout differential between root and tip.
*   **New Camber Profile (NACA 24xx)**: The top designs use either NACA 44xx or 00xx. To foster novelty, I will explore the NACA 24xx series (2% max camber at 40% chord). This provides a moderate amount of camber, less aggressive than 44xx, which might offer a better CL/CDi ratio by slightly increasing lift without incurring excessive drag penalties, especially with a thinner profile.
*   **Mid-Range Thickness**: High-performing designs had thicknesses of 9% (D3) and 13% (D1). Combining a moderate camber profile with a mid-range thickness (e.g., 10-11%) can offer a good balance between structural requirements and aerodynamic efficiency (reducing parasitic drag compared to thicker airfoils).
*   **Low Dihedral**: Extremes of dihedral (e.g., D4's 9.4 deg) were detrimental. Maintaining a low, slightly negative dihedral (as seen in D3) helps lateral stability without significantly impacting CL/CDi.
