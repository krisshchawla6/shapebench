*   **Apply Exploration Strategy**: The primary goal is to push into completely underexplored regions of the design space, rather than making incremental changes to existing high-performers. We will target boundary conditions for several parameters.
*   **Leverage Sweep Potential**: High sweep generally correlates with higher rewards. To explore the limits of this, we will choose a very high `le_sweep` near the upper boundary (e.g., 78.0 degrees) to maximize vortex lift potential, as designs above 70 degrees are sparse.
*   **Explore Large Scale**: The current population hasn't explored the largest possible wings. We will select a `root_chord_in` near the upper boundary (e.g., 48.0 inches) to test if a larger wing area, despite potential drag penalties, can lead to a higher CL/CDi ratio by generating more total lift.
*   **Novel Twist Distribution**: All high-reward designs feature significant washout (negative twist). We will explore a highly unconventional twist setup: a small *positive* `twist_root` combined with a strongly *negative* `twist_tip` (washout). This creates a very aggressive load distribution change from root to tip, which could be detrimental or, if vortex interaction is favorable, surprisingly effective. This directly addresses the unexplored positive twist range.
*   **Extreme Thinness**: No designs have explored the thinnest airfoils. We will choose `naca_t=6` (the thinnest possible) to investigate the effect of minimizing profile drag and its interaction with the other extreme parameters.
*   **Strong Positive Dihedral**: Most successful designs had near-zero or slight anhedral. To explore another boundary, we will select a significant *positive* `dihedral` (e.g., 12.0 degrees). This will test its impact on stability and performance in combination with high sweep and aggressive twist.
*   **Camber**: We will retain some camber, using `NACA 24` to maintain lift characteristics, balancing it against the extreme thinness and twist choices. This provides some known benefit while other parameters are highly exploratory.
