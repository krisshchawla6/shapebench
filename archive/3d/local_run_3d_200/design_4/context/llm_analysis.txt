Therefore, this analysis will address general principles for designing a delta wing aimed at maximizing CL/CDi:

A high CL/CDi ratio in delta wings typically arises from a strong, stable leading-edge vortex system providing significant vortex lift, combined with an optimized spanwise load distribution to minimize induced drag. Profile drag must also be kept low.
*   **Ideal Characteristics**: Strong, well-attached leading-edge vortices across the operating angle of attack range. Lift distributed across the span to minimize induced drag (often achieved through twist). A thin airfoil section with moderate camber to balance lift and drag.
*   **Potential Issues (general to sub-optimal delta wings)**:
    1.  **Premature Vortex Breakdown**: If sweep is too low or the design is otherwise poor, vortices can detach or burst too early, leading to a sudden loss of lift and increased drag.
    2.  **High Induced Drag**: Sub-optimal spanwise lift distribution, often with excessive loading near the wingtips, can significantly increase induced drag. This can be mitigated with appropriate twist.
    3.  **Excessive Profile Drag**: Thick airfoils or poorly chosen camber distributions can lead to high form drag and skin friction, reducing the overall CL/CDi.
    4.  **Stall Behavior**: Abrupt or asymmetric stall can occur if the flow separates uncontrollably, particularly at the tips.

Given the lack of previous designs, the "Exploitation Strategy" will be applied by designing a robust initial candidate based on fundamental aerodynamic principles known to yield good CL/CDi for delta wings. This design will serve as a strong baseline for future evolutionary steps.
