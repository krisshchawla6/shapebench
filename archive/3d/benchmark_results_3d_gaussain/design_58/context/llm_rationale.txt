*   **Novel High Sweep Exploration:** The current top designs predominantly feature moderate sweep angles (45-55 degrees). To explore a novel region of the design space and leverage different aerodynamic principles (e.g., stronger, more stable leading-edge vortices), I will significantly increase the leading-edge sweep to `68.0` degrees. This pushes into a realm where vortex lift is a much more dominant lift-generating mechanism.
*   **Smoother Twist Distribution for High Sweep:** The aggressive washout (large positive root twist, max negative tip twist) of the top designs is likely optimized for lower sweep configurations. With higher sweep and dominant vortex lift, an overly aggressive twist might lead to premature vortex breakdown or inefficient lift distribution. I will opt for a much smoother, less extreme twist distribution: `twist_root=3.0` and `twist_tip=-3.0`. This aims for a more uniform spanwise loading distribution or one that gently encourages the formation and stability of leading-edge vortices.
*   **Increased Root Chord for Aspect Ratio:** As sweep increases, the geometric aspect ratio decreases significantly. To counteract this and ensure sufficient wing area for lift generation, while also potentially maintaining a more favorable effective aspect ratio, I will increase the `root_chord_in` to `35.0` inches. This is on the higher side of the explored range, providing a larger overall planform with the high sweep.
*   **Minimal Thickness for Drag Reduction:** To complement the high sweep and potentially reduce profile drag, especially at higher speeds where such wings might operate, I will specify the thinnest allowed airfoil: `naca_t=6`. This will be combined with a standard cambered profile (`naca_m=2`, `naca_p=4`) to still provide some lift at zero angle of attack, but with minimal thickness penalty.
*   **Moderate Positive Dihedral:** The top designs show a consistent preference for positive dihedral. I will maintain a moderate positive `dihedral=7.0` to ensure good lateral stability without being excessively high, which could potentially interact unfavorably with strong leading-edge vortices.
