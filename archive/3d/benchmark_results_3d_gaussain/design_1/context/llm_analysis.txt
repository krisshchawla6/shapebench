A generic delta wing, especially if it's untwisted and uncambered, often exhibits the following aerodynamic characteristics:
*   **Issues**: Lift distribution heavily concentrated at the root, leading to strong tip vortices and high induced drag. Potentially inadequate lift at lower angles of attack due to lack of camber. Without washout, there's a risk of tip stall at higher angles, or inefficient lift generation across the span. The reward (CL/CDi) would likely be suboptimal due to these inefficiencies.
*   **Sweep Impact**: High sweep (e.g., >60 degrees) is essential for developing stable leading-edge vortices, which enhance lift, but also contributes to higher induced drag.
*   **Twist Impact**: An untwisted wing will have a less efficient spanwise lift distribution. Washout (negative twist at the tip relative to the root) is crucial for shedding a more uniform wake, reducing induced drag, and managing stall characteristics.
*   **Camber Impact**: Zero camber (NACA m=0) means the airfoil generates less lift at a given angle of attack compared to a cambered airfoil, requiring higher angles for the same lift, which can increase drag.
*   **Thickness Impact**: A moderate thickness (e.g., 12-18%) typically balances structural requirements with acceptable profile drag. Very thin wings might lack structural integrity, while very thick ones can incur high profile drag.
*   **Key changes needed to improve designs**:
    1.  Introduce a moderate washout to improve spanwise lift distribution and reduce induced drag.
    2.  Explore moderate camber to increase lift efficiency and potentially lower angles of attack for cruise.
    3.  Vary sweep in the upper-mid range (e.g., 60-75 degrees) to leverage vortex lift effectively.
    4.  Ensure a reasonable root chord and thickness for overall wing scale and structural viability.
