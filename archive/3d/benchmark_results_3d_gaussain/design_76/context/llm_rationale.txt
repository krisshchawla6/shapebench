*   **Explore High Sweep**: The current top designs are all clustered at the lowest end of the `le_sweep` range (45-48 degrees). To explore the design space effectively and potentially discover higher performance regions, we must venture into significantly higher sweep angles. A sweep of around 70 degrees represents a substantially different aerodynamic regime, where vortex lift becomes even more dominant, and other parameters might need different optimization strategies.
*   **Investigate Symmetric Airfoils**: All high-ranking designs utilize NACA 24XX series airfoils. Introducing a symmetric airfoil (NACA 00XX) allows us to isolate the effects of twist and overall wing geometry on lift generation, rather than relying on inherent airfoil camber. This will test if the twist alone can manage the lift distribution efficiently, especially with high sweep.
*   **Introduce Negative Dihedral**: All current designs feature positive dihedral. Exploring negative dihedral (tips down) is an under-explored region that could significantly impact roll stability and the interaction of leading-edge vortices, especially in conjunction with high sweep and a symmetric airfoil. This could uncover novel aerodynamic behaviors.
*   **Moderate Twist for High Sweep/Symmetric Airfoil**: With a symmetric airfoil and high sweep, the optimal twist distribution might be less aggressive than the extreme washout observed in the low-sweep, cambered designs. A moderate positive `twist_root` and negative `twist_tip` will still provide washout for load management but will explore a less extreme twist combination for this new geometric configuration.
*   **Higher Root Chord**: The current designs typically use root chords in the 25-40 inch range. To explore the scale effects in conjunction with high sweep and a novel configuration, increasing the `root_chord_in` towards the upper limit (e.g., 45-50 inches) can provide insights into how size interacts with the other chosen parameters.
*   **Moderate Thickness**: The `naca_t` parameter has been around 8-14. Using a slightly thicker airfoil (e.g., 18%) for a highly swept wing with a symmetric profile can assess the impact of structural depth and profile drag in this new aerodynamic context.
