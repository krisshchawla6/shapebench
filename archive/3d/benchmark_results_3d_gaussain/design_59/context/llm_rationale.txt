*   **Aggressively Exploring Higher Sweep (`le_sweep` = 65.0):** The current population has converged on low sweep angles (45-48 deg) for optimal performance, with higher sweeps generally yielding poor results. To genuinely *explore* the design space and find new optima, this design pushes into a significantly higher sweep region (middle of the 45-80 range). This tests a different aerodynamic regime where vortex lift becomes more pronounced, requiring potentially different parameter combinations for efficiency.
*   **Introducing Anhedral (`dihedral` = -10.0) for Radical Departure:** All top-performing designs utilize high positive dihedral. This design deliberately explores the opposite extreme (anhedral or negative dihedral), which is an entirely unexplored region. This bold move aims to understand its impact on aerodynamic efficiency and stability, potentially uncovering unexpected performance characteristics, especially when combined with high sweep and camber.
*   **Testing Higher Camber NACA (`naca_m` = 4, `naca_p` = 4):** All high-ranking designs use `NACA 24xx`. This design incorporates `NACA 44xx` (4% max camber at 40% chord), which provides significantly more camber. This explores whether increased camber can provide more lift, allowing for a lower angle of attack and potentially reducing induced drag for a given lift requirement, especially beneficial on a highly swept wing.
*   **Adjusting Twist to Complement High Sweep and Camber:** With a higher sweep and increased camber, the wing's inherent lift distribution changes. A moderate positive `twist_root` (`3.0` deg) and a substantial but not maximal `twist_tip` washout (`-7.0` deg) are chosen. This combination aims to manage the altered spanwise load distribution effectively, preventing premature tip stall while maintaining an efficient lift profile, diverging from the extreme positive root twist and max washout of the current top designs.
*   **Exploring Larger Scale and Thickness (`root_chord_in` = 40.0, `naca_t` = 18):** Top designs cluster around mid-range root chords (25-33 inches) and thickness (9-14%). This design pushes both parameters towards the higher end of their ranges. A larger root chord increases the overall scale and planform area, influencing Reynolds number effects, while a thicker airfoil explores its impact on form drag and potential for higher lift generation, particularly in conjunction with increased camber.
