**Geometry Observations (Design 1):**
*   **Classic Camber:** The shape closely resembles a standard subsonic airfoil (e.g., NACA 4-series) with a well-defined rounded leading edge and a tapered sharp trailing edge.
*   **Smooth Curvature:** The upper surface (CP1) arcs smoothly without inflections or lumps, indicating effective use of the `edgy_param` (-0.93) to maintain continuity.
*   **Thickness Distribution:** The airfoil has a moderate thickness ratio (estimated ~12-15%), with the maximum thickness located around 30-40% of the chord.
*   **Asymmetry:** The design is clearly asymmetric (cambered), with the leading edge (CP0) and trailing edge (CP2) vertically offset to generate lift.

**Flow Field Observations (Inferred from Rank 1):**
*   **Attached Flow:** The high reward (5.19) implies the flow remains attached over most of the surface. There are likely no massive separation bubbles.
*   **Suction Peak:** A significant low-pressure region (suction peak) exists near the leading edge on the upper surface, generating the bulk of the lift.
*   **Clean Wake:** The wake trace is likely thin and steady, indicating low pressure drag and a stable Kutta condition at the sharp trailing edge.
*   **Pressure Recovery:** The pressure gradient from the peak suction to the trailing edge is gradual enough to prevent boundary layer separation.

**Key Design Changes Needed:**
*   **Load Distribution:** Design 1 is front-loaded. To improve, we should explore **rear-loading** (pushing thickness/camber further back) to potentially extend the region of laminar flow or favorable pressure gradient.
*   **Leading Edge Attitude:** The current LE is fairly neutral. A **drooped leading edge** could improve performance by aligning the stagnation point better for high-lift conditions or reducing the suction peak intensity.
*   **Thickness Management:** Explore a slightly thinner profile or different thickness-to-camber ratio to minimize form drag while maintaining lift.
