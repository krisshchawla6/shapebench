*   **Geometry (from Design 1 images)**: The visualized design features a classic thin airfoil profile with a relatively flat bottom and a curved top surface. The leading edge (LE) appears clean with a small radius, transitioning smoothly into the upper surface. The trailing edge (TE) tapers to a sharp point. There are no visible pinch points, but the profile is quite slender, which may limit lift capacity compared to a thicker, higher-camber variant.
*   **Flow Fields**: The pressure distribution (`1_p.png`) shows a classic high-pressure stagnation zone at the nose and a low-pressure suction zone over the forward 30% of the upper surface. The recovery region seems smooth. The velocity fields (`1_u.png`, `1_v.png`) show a narrow, stable wake, indicating attached flow. There is no evidence of massive flow separation, which aligns with the high reward.
*   **Mapping**: The high reward of Design 1 correlates with `r0` being small (~0.026) and `e2` being 1.0 (sharp TE). The failure cases (Designs 4 & 5) clearly demonstrate that excessive `r` values (0.5+) create blob-like non-aerodynamic shapes that ruin the flow. The "Edgy" parameter `e` works best when -1 (smooth) for curvature and 1 (sharp) for the trailing edge.
*   **Key Changes Needed**:
    1.  Shift the maximum thickness point further aft to extend the region of favorable pressure gradient (laminar flow strategy).
    2.  Slightly increase the aft camber to boost lift without inducing separation.
    3.  Maintain the strict "small radius" constraint at the leading edge to prevent drag spikes.
