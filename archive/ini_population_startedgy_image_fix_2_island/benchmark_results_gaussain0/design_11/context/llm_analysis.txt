    - The overall profile is very thin, with the maximum thickness located relatively far forward.
    - The trailing edge appears to close cleanly, but the lower surface shows a slight flattening or subtle concavity toward the rear half.
    - There is a noticeable "kink" in curvature at the leading edge where the upper and lower surfaces meet, suggesting a lack of a proper leading-edge radius.

- **Flow Field / Simulation Results**:
    - **Pressure Plot**: Shows a massive, concentrated low-pressure (blue) spike exactly at the sharp leading edge. This indicates a stagnation point transition that is too abrupt, which usually leads to early flow separation at higher angles of attack.
    - **Velocity (U)**: The flow remains mostly attached over the upper surface, but the wake thickness suggests some energy loss.
    - **Velocity (V)**: Shows strong upwash at the nose and downwash at the tail, confirming lift generation, but the intensity at the nose is localized due to the sharpness.

- **Mapping & Key Changes**:
    - Design 1's action vector shows `e0 = 1.0` (edgy) and `r0 = -0.76`. This high "edgy" parameter is clearly responsible for the sharp, problematic leading edge spike.
    - The reward is high (1.1645), meaning the thin profile is aerodynamic, but the "spike" in pressure is a failure point for efficiency.
    - **Key Changes Needed**: 
        1. Significantly reduce the `edgy_param` at the leading edge to create a rounded nose radius.
        2. Increase the `radius_param` of the upper control point to slightly thicken the airfoil for better pressure distribution.
        3. Adjust the lower surface parameters to eliminate the slight concavity and ensure a smoother pressure recovery.
