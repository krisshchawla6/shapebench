*   **Explore lower sweep angles for diverse aerodynamics:** Most designs (especially Design 1) are in the 65-68 degree sweep range. To advance population diversity and creativity, I will explore a significantly lower sweep angle (e.g., 55 degrees). This could fundamentally change the vortex shedding characteristics, reduce vortex strength but potentially lower induced drag, and shift the effective aspect ratio, seeking a different aerodynamic optimum.
*   **Investigate unconventional twist distribution (wash-in at root):** Current successful designs feature washout. I will try a "wash-in" at the root (positive twist) combined with washout at the tip (negative twist). This creates a very different load distribution, aiming to distribute lift further inboard and potentially delay stall at lower sweep angles, offering a bold departure from existing trends.
*   **Simplify airfoil to isolate planform/twist effects:** To understand the pure aerodynamic contribution of the novel sweep and twist combination, I will use a `NACA 00xx` symmetric airfoil (`naca_m=0, naca_p=0`). This removes the variable of camber from the equation, allowing for clearer insights into the interaction of the primary geometric parameters.
*   **Test extreme thickness for structural/stall resilience:** While thinner airfoils generally reduce drag, I will specify a relatively thick airfoil (`naca_t=20`). This explores the upper range of the thickness parameter, which could yield advantages in terms of structural integrity or different high-angle-of-attack behavior, challenging the common assumption that only thin airfoils are optimal for performance.
*   **Explore smaller scale with reduced root chord:** Design 1 has a mid-range root chord (34.8 inches), and larger chords often appear in lower-reward designs. I will select a significantly smaller root chord (e.g., 18.0 inches) to investigate the impact of overall wing scale and aspect ratio at these new sweep and twist parameters, potentially reducing wetted area and skin friction drag.
