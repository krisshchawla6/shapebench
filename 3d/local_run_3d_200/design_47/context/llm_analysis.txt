The leading design (Design 1) achieves a remarkably high reward of 4016.0090, significantly outperforming all other designs. Its parameters include a moderate-high leading-edge sweep (65.5 degrees), a root chord of 34.8 inches, moderate washout at the root (-7.1 degrees) and less at the tip (-4.6 degrees), slight negative dihedral (-1.7 degrees), and a NACA 4412 airfoil (4% max camber at 40% chord, 12% thickness).

From the Cp distribution image, Design 1 exhibits a strong, well-formed leading-edge vortex, indicated by the prominent low-pressure region (dark blue/purple) along the leading edge and extending across a substantial portion of the upper wing surface. This strong vortex lift is clearly a major contributor to its high CL. The gradual pressure recovery towards the trailing edge and the lack of severe pressure gradients suggest a relatively stable flow with good lift generation and potentially low induced drag relative to the lift. The washout appears to be effectively distributing the lift, preventing premature tip stall.

Key changes needed to improve designs in the population and explore novelty:
1.  **Vary Sweep Extremes:** Explore both higher (e.g., >70 degrees) and lower (e.g., <60 degrees) sweep angles to understand their impact on vortex strength and induced drag.
2.  **Camber Profile Diversification:** Move away from the NACA 44XX and 24XX series that dominate the top designs. Experiment with zero camber (NACA 00XX) or different camber positions (NACA X0XX, though not an option with naca_p=0 or 4).
3.  **Twist Distribution Exploration:** Systematically vary the differential between root and tip twist, including scenarios where tip washout is more aggressive relative to the root, or where the absolute washout values are pushed to their limits.
4.  **Thickness Impact:** Explore the full range of thickness values, particularly at the higher end (NACA XX20-XX24), to see its interaction with other parameters, especially sweep and camber.
