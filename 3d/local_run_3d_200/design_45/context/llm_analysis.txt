Key observations from the population:
*   **Sweep**: Higher sweep angles (63-68 deg) correlate with better performance. The lowest performing design (Design 5) had a very low sweep (50.4 deg). This suggests that high sweep is critical, likely due to enhanced vortex lift.
*   **Twist**: All higher-performing designs (Rank 0-3) utilized significant negative twist (washout) at the root, often at the boundary of -10.0 deg. The twist at the tip was also consistently negative, contributing to overall washout. Design 5, with very little root twist (-1.2 deg), performed poorly, indicating the importance of washout for load distribution and possibly vortex stability.
*   **Camber**: Cambered airfoils (NACA 24xx, 44xx) generally performed better than symmetric (NACA 00xx), though a NACA 0011 design (Design 4) still achieved a moderate rank. The best design used NACA 4412.
*   **Thickness**: Thickness values ranged from 11 to 19, with the best design using 12. No designs explored the extreme ends (6 or 24).
*   **Dihedral**: Most designs had dihedral close to zero or slightly negative. Design 5 had a significant anhedral (-9.0 deg), contributing to its poor performance.

Issues and potential improvements based on current population trends:
1.  The very high sweep range (70-80 deg) and very low sweep range (45-55 deg) are underexplored. Higher sweep likely improves vortex lift but could increase induced drag or structural challenges.
2.  The maximum root chord (37-50 inches) is largely unexplored. A larger chord means more wing area, potentially increasing lift but also total drag.
3.  Extreme thickness values (NACA 6-8 or 22-24) have not been tested. Thinner airfoils might reduce profile drag, while thicker ones could offer more internal volume and structural rigidity.
4.  The parameter space for twist, particularly positive twist values for `twist_root`, is entirely unexplored. While washout generally seems beneficial, extreme or unconventional twist distributions might uncover new optima or clarify the sensitivity.
