The top-performing design (Design 1, Reward: 424.5627) features a high leading-edge sweep (~71 deg), a mid-range root chord (~32.9 in), a uniform strong negative twist (-10 deg root and tip), maximum negative dihedral (-15 deg), and a relatively thin NACA 2407 airfoil. Key observations from the data:
*   **High Sweep & Moderate Chord:** A sweep around 70-71 degrees with a root chord around 32-33 inches appears optimal. Higher sweeps and larger chords (Designs 5, 6) generally lead to lower rewards if other parameters are not tuned.
*   **Strong Negative Twist / Washout:** The top designs exhibit significant negative twist. Design 1 shows uniform strong negative twist, while Design 3 has a classic washout configuration. This seems crucial for effective lift distribution and possibly vortex management. Designs with wash-in (Design 6) or complex twist leading to positive root twist (Design 5) perform poorly.
*   **Strong Negative Dihedral:** The highest reward is associated with maximum negative dihedral (-15 degrees). Milder negative dihedral (Design 2) results in significantly lower rewards. This suggests a potential interaction with vortex lift or stability at the operational angle of attack.
*   **Thin NACA 24xx Airfoil:** A NACA 2407 or 2408 profile (moderate camber, camber at 40% chord, thin) appears consistently in high-reward designs. Thicker airfoils (2411 in Design 2) or unusual camber distributions (4010 in Design 4) generally reduce performance.

Based on this, the primary aerodynamic issues in lower-performing designs are likely related to:
1.  **Suboptimal Lift Distribution:** Incorrect twist and dihedral settings can lead to inefficient lift generation and higher induced drag.
2.  **Increased Drag:** Thicker airfoils contribute to higher profile drag, and some camber choices might also lead to higher form or pressure drag.
3.  **Ineffective Vortex Management:** Delta wings rely heavily on vortex lift. The combination of sweep, twist, and dihedral likely plays a significant role in establishing stable and strong leading-edge vortices.

**Key design changes needed to improve designs in the population:**
1.  Systematically explore the range of strong negative twist/washout, from uniform to progressive.
2.  Confirm the efficacy of maximum negative dihedral by testing similar configurations.
3.  Maintain airfoil profiles within the thin NACA 24xx series, further refining thickness.
4.  Consistently explore the high-sweep, moderate-chord region that yielded top results.
