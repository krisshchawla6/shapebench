The current population is heavily biased towards high sweep, aggressive washout, and negative dihedral. To promote diversity and discover potentially overlooked optima, I will explore a design that challenges these dominant trends while aiming for a good CL/CDi.

*   **Explore lower sweep angles (e.g., ~55 degrees):** The top designs are all high sweep (70+ degrees). This design will investigate a significantly lower sweep to assess its potential when combined with other optimized parameters. Lower sweep can reduce induced drag and potentially allow for more efficient conventional lift, but might weaken vortex lift. This is a bold departure from the current high-reward cluster.
*   **Introduce positive dihedral:** All high-reward designs utilize negative dihedral. This design will experiment with a positive dihedral (+10.0 degrees) to explore its impact on lateral stability and potential interaction with vortex flow for a lower-sweep delta wing, a significant deviation from current successful designs.
*   **Utilize aggressive camber (NACA 44xx):** Top designs use NACA 00xx or 24xx. I will employ a NACA 4410 airfoil to explore the effect of significant camber in combination with lower sweep and positive dihedral. This aims to generate lift more directly, potentially complementing or substituting for strong vortex lift in a lower sweep configuration.
*   **Moderate, asymmetric washout:** I will use a moderate washout (-5 degrees at root, -8 degrees at tip). While still providing washout for load distribution benefits, it's less extreme than the -10/-10 seen in top designs. This will allow for a different lift distribution, tailored for the lower sweep and cambered airfoil.
*   **Moderate thickness (NACA 4410):** Choosing a thickness of 10% is a middle-ground approach. It's thicker than the NACA 0006 of Design 1 but thinner than some of the poorer-performing designs, providing a robust test for this unconventional parameter combination.
*   **Maintain root chord:** A root chord of 30.0 inches keeps the wing within a reasonable scale without introducing extreme size variations that might skew results, allowing focus on the other geometric changes.
