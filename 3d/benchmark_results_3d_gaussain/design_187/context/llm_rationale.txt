Our current population has converged on a strategy involving high sweep, strong washout, maximum anhedral, and very thin symmetric airfoils. While successful, this leaves vast regions of the parameter space unexplored. To advance the population and discover novel high-performing designs, we must employ an **exploration strategy**, focusing on boundary regions and distinct parameter combinations.

*   **Exploring Lower Sweep (55.0 deg)**: The current best designs are all above 70 degrees sweep. By setting `le_sweep` significantly lower, to 55.0 degrees, we explore a regime where vortex lift might be less dominant, but attached flow and potentially lower induced drag from a higher effective aspect ratio could be leveraged. This is a significant deviation from the current high-reward cluster.
*   **Testing Maximum Positive Dihedral (15.0 deg)**: All high-performing designs exhibit maximum anhedral (-15.0 deg). Applying maximum positive dihedral (tips up) is a radical departure. This will drastically change the wing's lateral stability characteristics and its interaction with the local flow, potentially revealing a completely new aerodynamic response, even if initially suboptimal. This pushes a parameter to its extreme boundary, which is a key aspect of exploration.
*   **Utilizing a Large Root Chord (45.0 in)**: The `root_chord_in` parameter is consistently mid-range in top designs. By choosing a large root chord of 45.0 inches, near its maximum limit, we create a much larger wing area. This change in scale will interact with the lower sweep and positive dihedral in an underexplored way, potentially altering Reynolds number effects and overall lift generation capacity.
*   **Maintaining Aggressive Washout (-10.0 deg root, -10.0 deg tip) with Moderate Camber (NACA 2412)**: While we are aggressively exploring sweep and dihedral, retaining the aggressive washout (max negative twist at both root and tip) is a common feature of successful designs. This aims to maintain an efficient load distribution and prevent tip stall. We'll use a cambered airfoil (NACA 24) and a slightly thicker profile (`naca_t=12`) to see if this combination can provide a lift advantage or better flow management in the context of lower sweep and positive dihedral, rather than the very thin symmetric airfoils. This also explores `naca_t` values outside the currently common 6-9 range.
