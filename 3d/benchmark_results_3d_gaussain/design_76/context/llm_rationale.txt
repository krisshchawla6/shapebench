*   **Novel Design Strategy:** The current high-reward designs leverage high sweep, strong washout, and negative dihedral. To explore new, high-potential regions, I will maintain these general trends but push certain parameters towards their limits or explore variations within the effective range, rather than mimicking specific values.
*   **Maximizing Vortex Lift (le_sweep):** Designs with higher sweep have shown better performance. I will explore a significantly higher sweep angle, pushing close to the upper bound (e.g., 78-79 degrees), to maximize the vortex lift contribution, which is crucial for delta wings. This is a direct exploration of the performance envelope.
*   **Refined Washout (twist_root, twist_tip):** High-reward designs heavily utilize washout. Instead of an identical, extreme washout at root and tip as in Design 1, I will implement a strong washout, but with a slight gradient (e.g., more at the root, less at the tip, but both strongly negative). This aims to achieve an efficient spanwise load distribution by delaying root stall and ensuring the tips maintain lift.
*   **Slightly Moderated Dihedral:** While extreme negative dihedral correlated with high rewards, I will slightly moderate it from the absolute maximum (e.g., -15.0 to -10.0). This still provides the benefits of anhedral for lateral stability and effective sweep but moves away from the absolute boundary, exploring if the peak performance can be maintained or improved without the most extreme value.
*   **Exploring Airfoil Thickness (naca_t):** The top designs mostly use NACA 2407-2411. I will retain the camber (naca_m=2, naca_p=4) as it seems beneficial, but experiment with a notably thicker airfoil (e.g., naca_t=14). This could provide structural advantages and potentially alter boundary layer behavior beneficially for a highly swept wing.
*   **Intermediate Root Chord:** The top designs had root chords around 30-33 inches. I will choose a slightly larger, yet still moderate, root chord (e.g., 38.0 inches). This increases the overall scale of the wing, potentially leading to higher lift coefficients, while still being within a reasonable range.
