The current top designs are clustered around high sweep, strong washout, and maximum anhedral. To advance the population and explore the design space effectively, I will target a drastically different region with the following reasoning:

*   **Explore significantly lower sweep (e.g., ~50-55 deg)**: All high-reward designs cluster around 69-72 degrees. Design 5 at 80 degrees performed poorly. A lower sweep will reduce vortex lift but potentially decrease induced drag at certain conditions and offer different handling characteristics. This is a major departure from the high-performing set.
*   **Investigate positive twist (washin) at the root and mild positive twist at the tip**: All successful designs utilize strong washout. Exploring washin at the root, potentially combined with slight washin or very mild washout at the tip, will fundamentally alter the load distribution and may reveal a new pathway to high CL/CDi, or at least help map the design space boundaries.
*   **Test positive dihedral (tips up)**: All high-reward designs feature maximum anhedral (-15 degrees). Dihedral has a significant impact on lateral stability and can affect induced drag. A strong positive dihedral (e.g., +10 degrees) is a direct opposite of the current trend and will provide valuable data on this unexplored region.
*   **Utilize maximum camber (`naca_m=4, naca_p=4`) and a thicker airfoil (`naca_t > 10`)**: The `naca_m=4` profile has not been explored in the top designs. Combining this with a moderate thickness (e.g., 16-18%) could lead to a different lift curve slope or better performance at lower angles of attack compared to the thinner, less cambered profiles seen so far.
*   **Increase root chord significantly**: Most successful designs hover around 30-33 inches. A much larger root chord (e.g., 40-50 inches) combined with a lower sweep will create a wing with a different aspect ratio and potentially different flow characteristics, which has not been explored at the higher end of the range.
