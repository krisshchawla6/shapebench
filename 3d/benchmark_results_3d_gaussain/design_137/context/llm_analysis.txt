The current top-ranked designs (especially Design 1, reward 424.56) exhibit a consistent pattern:
*   **High leading-edge sweep** (around 70-76 degrees): This is characteristic of delta wings designed to generate strong leading-edge vortices for lift at higher angles of attack.
*   **Aggressive washout** (twist_root and twist_tip near -10 degrees): This negative twist helps to offload the wing tips, delay tip stall, and improve spanwise lift distribution, which reduces induced drag (critical for CL/CDi).
*   **Maximum negative dihedral** (-15 degrees): Tips are pointing significantly down. This configuration typically reduces effective dihedral, potentially improving roll responsiveness or influencing vortex formation/stability, though its exact aerodynamic benefit in this context requires further study.
*   **Cambered airfoils (NACA 24xx)**: The top designs utilize 2% camber, which provides additional lift compared to symmetric airfoils (like NACA 0008 in Design 3, which has a lower reward).
*   **Moderate thickness** (NACA 7-8% chord): Design 1 uses NACA 2407, suggesting thinner airfoils might be more efficient for these high-sweep configurations.

**Aerodynamic Issues (Inferred from common delta wing behavior and population clustering):**
Given the high rewards, these designs likely achieve good vortex lift and manage induced drag well due to the washout. However, a potential issue with such extreme parameters (max sweep, max washout, max negative dihedral) is that the design space around them might be narrow, or this specific combination might only be optimal for a very specific flight condition. The lack of diversity suggests that the optimizer has converged on a local optimum, potentially missing broader regions of the design space.

1.  **Explore significantly lower sweep angles:** The current designs are all clustered above 69 degrees. Lower sweeps (e.g., 45-65 degrees) would test designs less reliant on strong leading-edge vortices, potentially revealing new lift generation strategies.
2.  **Investigate positive or neutral dihedral:** All high-reward designs use negative dihedral. Exploring positive dihedral would drastically change the wing's lateral stability characteristics and vortex interaction.
3.  **Vary twist distribution beyond aggressive washout:** Test less extreme washout, no twist, or even positive twist at the root, to see how load distribution changes affect efficiency.
