The most successful designs (e.g., Design 1, reward 626.7341) exhibit a consistent pattern: very high leading-edge sweep (around 77-78 degrees), significant negative twist (washout) at both root and tip (often near the -10.0 degree limit), and extreme negative dihedral (-15.0 degrees). These designs also tend to use relatively thin airfoils (NACA 0006 or 0007). The dominant aerodynamic strategy appears to leverage strong vortex lift from the highly swept leading edge, with washout optimizing lift distribution to delay tip stall and minimize induced drag, and anhedral potentially contributing to roll stability characteristics.

Key issues that might be implicitly addressed by these parameters:
*   **High Induced Drag**: Inherent to highly swept, low aspect ratio wings. The washout strategy aims to mitigate this by optimizing spanwise lift distribution.
*   **Vortex Breakdown**: High sweep helps generate stable leading-edge vortices, but their integrity is crucial for lift. The precise twist distribution likely helps maintain stable vortices over the wing.
*   **Lateral Stability**: The strong anhedral might be compensating for the inherent positive effective dihedral of highly swept wings, or targeting specific roll response.

Key design changes needed to improve designs in the population, based on current patterns:

1.  **Explore the boundaries of `le_sweep`**: While high sweep is favored, designs haven't consistently hit the absolute maximum of 80.0 degrees combined with other boundary conditions.
2.  **Challenge the `dihedral` bias**: Almost all high-reward designs feature extreme negative dihedral (-15.0). The positive dihedral range (0.0 to 15.0) is virtually unexplored, representing a significant opportunity for discovery.
3.  **Investigate thicker and maximally cambered airfoils**: The top designs use thin symmetric airfoils (NACA 0006/0007) or slightly cambered ones (NACA 24xx). Combinations of maximum camber (NACA 44xx) with thicker profiles (e.g., `naca_t` > 10) are underexplored and could yield different lift characteristics.
4.  **Experiment with positive twist (wash-in)**: All successful designs use negative twist. Exploring positive twist, especially at the root, or even at the tip, could reveal entirely different lift distribution behaviors, although it's a high-risk exploration.
