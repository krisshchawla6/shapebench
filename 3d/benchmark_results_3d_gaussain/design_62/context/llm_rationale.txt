*   The current population's success is heavily concentrated in a specific region of the design space (high sweep, heavy washout, anhedral, cambered airfoils). To advance the evolutionary search, a bold exploratory step into an underexplored region is necessary, especially testing the boundaries of the parameter space.
*   I will significantly **increase the leading-edge sweep to near the upper limit (78 degrees)**. This pushes the design into a region where vortex lift might be even more dominant, or where new aerodynamic phenomena (e.g., shock interaction at higher speeds, though not explicitly simulated here, it's a general aerodynamic exploration) might emerge, potentially unlocking new performance plateaus. This is a direct exploration of a boundary region for a key parameter.
*   Instead of the ubiquitous heavy washout, I will **introduce a mild washin at the root (+2.0 degrees) and a mild washout at the tip (-2.0 degrees)**. This dramatically different twist distribution aims to explore how a more nuanced load distribution, potentially concentrating lift more inboard while still having some outboard relief, affects CL/CDi, moving away from the highly negative twist values seen in the current best designs.
*   All top designs feature significant anhedral. I will **explore a moderate positive dihedral (10.0 degrees)**. This completely reverses the current trend, testing the impact of positive dihedral on stability and vortex behavior in conjunction with very high sweep, which is an entirely new configuration for high-reward designs.
*   While cambered airfoils proved beneficial (NACA 24xx), I will **increase the camber further by using NACA 4412 (naca_m=4, naca_p=4, naca_t=12)**. This explores a higher lift coefficient potential from the airfoil itself and a slightly thicker section compared to the prevalent 07-08% thickness, which could offer structural benefits or different aerodynamic characteristics not yet fully understood in this delta wing context.
*   The root chord in top designs hovers around 30-35 inches. I will **increase the root chord to 45 inches** to explore the effects of a larger overall wing area in combination with these extreme geometric parameters, testing if scaling up these boundary-pushing features can yield better results or reveal new design sensitivities.
