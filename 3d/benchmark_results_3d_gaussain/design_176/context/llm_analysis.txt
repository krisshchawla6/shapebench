Design 1 is a highly swept delta wing (le_sweep=77.8 deg) with a relatively small root chord (27.3 inches). It features significant washout (twist_root=-9.55, twist_tip=-9.13) and maximum negative dihedral (dihedral=-15.0). The airfoil is a very thin symmetric NACA 0006. This design achieved the highest reward of 626.7341.

From the Cp distribution, we observe strong leading-edge vortices, evidenced by the large regions of low pressure (blue) originating from the leading edge and extending over the wing surface. This indicates effective vortex lift generation, which is characteristic of highly swept delta wings. The pressure recovery towards the trailing edge seems reasonable.

Aerodynamic Issues & Observations:
*   **High Induced Drag**: While vortex lift is effective, very high sweep angles generally lead to higher induced drag components, especially at lower angles of attack where vortex lift is less dominant.
*   **Extreme Washout**: The aggressive negative twist at both root and tip might be reducing overall lift generation at the inboard sections, relying heavily on the outer leading-edge vortices. This could be optimizing the spanwise lift distribution for minimal induced drag *given* the high sweep and vortex characteristics, but it's a very specific solution.
*   **Maximum Negative Dihedral**: The -15 degree dihedral (anhedral) likely influences roll stability (making it less stable or more maneuverable) and may have an indirect effect on the vortex flow, but its direct contribution to CL/CDi is not immediately obvious from Cp. Its consistent presence in high-reward designs suggests it's beneficial.
*   **Thin Symmetric Airfoil**: NACA 0006 provides no inherent camber lift, relying entirely on angle of attack and vortex lift. Its thinness minimizes profile drag, which is generally good for CL/CDi, but might limit maximum lift.

Key Design Changes Needed to Improve Designs (in general population, not just Design 1):
1.  **Explore Moderate Sweep with Camber**: Investigate if a lower sweep angle (e.g., 60-70 degrees) combined with a highly cambered airfoil could yield a better CL/CDi by trading some vortex lift for more inherent lift and potentially lower induced drag from a higher effective aspect ratio.
2.  **Vary Twist Distribution Significantly**: Challenge the dominant trend of strong washout by exploring milder washout or even wash-in at the root, combined with moderate tip washout. This could alter the spanwise load distribution and vortex characteristics differently.
3.  **Investigate Positive Dihedral**: All top designs have negative dihedral. A bold exploration would be to test positive dihedral (dihedral > 0) to understand its impact on performance and stability in this design space.
4.  **Explore Thicker, Cambered Airfoils**: While thin symmetric airfoils perform well, exploring thicker, more cambered profiles (e.g., NACA 44xx with higher 't' values) could provide more structural volume and a different lift curve slope, potentially beneficial with other parameter changes.
