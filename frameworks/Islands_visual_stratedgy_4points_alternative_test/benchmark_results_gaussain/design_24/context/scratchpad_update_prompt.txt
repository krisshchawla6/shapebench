## Global Heuristics
*   **The Radius-Distance Law:** Radius acts as polar distance from centroid. Maximize LE ($r_0$) and TE ($r_2$) toward 1.0 to maximize chord length and aspect ratio [iter 7, 8, 24].
*   **The Volume Budget (Chord Collapse):** High radii on CP1/CP3 (>0.45) "steal" length from the chord. High mid-body radii force "Pebble" morphology [iter 21].
*   **The Waisted Trap:** Side radii < 0.40 under high chord tension creates "boomerang" profiles. CP1/CP3 must be $\ge$ 0.40 to maintain convexity [iter 11, 13, 17].
*   **The Hook/Fang Boundary:** High lower radius ($r_3 \ge 0.50$) and positive lower angle ($a_3 \ge 0.30$) trigger self-intersecting "fangs" [iter 22]. **Update:** High negative lower edginess ($e_3 < -0.90$) creates a concave "fang" cusp even at moderate angles ($a_3 \approx 0.20$), causing high-pressure drag [iter 24].
*   **The Closure Imbalance:** Aggressively reducing $r_1$ while maintaining high $r_3$ shifts the center of area downward, preventing TE closure and creating blunt vertical faces [iter 22].
*   **The Beaking Threshold:** $r_0 > 0.90$ creates a sharp "wedge" nose. Rounded subsonic stagnation requires $r_0 < 0.85$ [iter 13, 17, 21]. **Update:** $r_0 \approx 0.98$ maximizes chord but requires perfect stagnation alignment [iter 24].
*   **The Stagnation Alignment:** $a_0 \approx 0.0$ aligns the leading edge directly into the flow, minimizing frontal area and stagnation drag [iter 24].
*   **The Kutta Singularity:** TE Edgy ($e_2 = 1.0$) is mandatory. Orientation $a_2 \approx 0.0$ avoids "hooked" tail drag [iter 2, 11, 21, 24].

## Degree of Freedom Mapping

### CP0: Leading Edge (LE)
*   **Radius [0][0]:** Chord driver. 0.82 for stability; 0.98 for maximum chord extension [iter 21, 24].
*   **Angle [0][1]:** Controls vertical incidence. $a_0 \approx 0.0$ is the "exploitation move" for minimum frontal drag [iter 4, 24].
*   **Edgy [0][2]:** -1.0 for C-infinity continuity [iter 2].

### CP1: Upper Surface / Peak
*   **Radius [1][0]:** Thickness control. 0.35-0.40 is the "slender limit." Balances against $r_3$ for TE convergence [iter 8, 22, 24].
*   **Angle [1][1]:** Peak positioning. $a_1 \approx -0.42$ shifts peak thickness forward to ~25% chord, creating a long recovery ramp [iter 1, 17, 24].
*   **Edgy [1][2]:** Low values (-0.9) ensure smooth apex curvature and laminar acceleration [iter 4, 24].

### CP2: Trailing Edge (TE)
*   **Radius [2][0]:** Rearward extension. 1.0 maximizes aspect ratio. Neutralized if CP3 geometry forces bluntness [iter 7, 22, 24].
*   **Angle [2][1]:** Horizontal alignment; keep near 0.0 to prevent lift-induced drag at the tail [iter 4, 11, 22].
*   **Edgy [2][2]:** 1.0 enforces Kutta condition; critical for flow closure [iter 2, 21].

### CP3: Lower Surface
*   **Radius [3][0]:** Stability Anchor. ~0.45 is the "Concavity Floor." $r_3 > 0.50$ risks fangs [iter 17, 21, 22].
*   **Angle [3][1]:** Tuck control. $a_3 \approx 0.2$ is the limit for convexity. Higher values risk spline looping [iter 8, 17, 22].
*   **Edgy [3][2]:** **The Fang Driver.** High negative values ($e_3 \approx -0.95$) create sharp lower-LE concavities (fangs) that trap pressure and limit reward [iter 24]. Needs $e_3 > -0.8$ or higher $a_3$ to flatten the belly.

### Key Learnings

1.  **The "Stretching" Paradox**: Maximizing both $r_0$ (leading edge radius/extension) and $r_2$ (trailing edge radius/extension) to their limits (0.95 and 1.0) does not result in a super-long airfoil. Instead, it seems to break the geometric solver, leading to a "collapsed" or normalized shape that is much smaller and less efficient than Design 1.
2.  **The "Fang" is Resilient**: The prediction that $e_3 = -0.45$ would create a convex "belly" was incorrect. The resulting image shows a severe concave notch. This suggests that the "edginess" parameter ($e$) must likely be positive (or much closer to 0) to achieve true convexity on the lower surface.
3.  **Parameter Interaction (Coupling)**: The attempt to move the peak forward ($a_1 = -0.43$) while stretching the nose ($r_0 = 0.95$) created a "beaked" front end that effectively concentrated all the volume into the first 30% of the intended chord, leaving the rest of the geometry to fail or truncate.
4.  **Reward Sensitivity**: This design likely has a very low reward. The blunt trailing edge and the deep notch on the lower surface are catastrophic for drag. The "Pure Laminar" strategy failed because the parameters were pushed into a non-physical or unstable region of the design space.
