### Key Learnings

#### 1. The "Radius Inflation" Trap
You successfully avoided the "waisted" (concave) trap of previous designs by increasing $r_1$ and $r_3$. However, you fell into a new trap: **Global Radius Inflation**. When all four radius parameters ($r_0, r_1, r_2, r_3$) are high (0.8+), the control points are pushed nearly equidistant from the center. This transforms the airfoil from a long, slender wing into a blunt, low-aspect-ratio "pebble."

#### 2. The Slenderness Ratio Conflict
For an airfoil to generate high lift-to-drag, it generally needs to be much longer (chord) than it is thick. By setting $r_1$ (top) and $r_3$ (bottom) to values nearly as high as $r_0$ and $r_2$ (front/back), you forced the thickness to be nearly equal to the chord. 
*   **Correction**: To maintain a "Thick-Convex" profile that still functions as a wing, $r_1$ and $r_3$ should be significantly lower than the chord-defining $r_0$ and $r_2$ (e.g., $r_1, r_3 \approx 0.3-0.5$).

#### 3. Geometric Singularities vs. Proportions
Your prediction that $r_0=0.88$ would avoid "beaking" was correct. However, in this parameter space, the "beaking" or "waisting" seen in earlier designs is actually a symptom of the optimizer trying to achieve extreme slenderness. By moving to a fully convex shape, you have improved structural "sanity" but lost the high-speed flow characteristics required for a high reward.

#### 4. Forward Loading ($a_1$) in a Stubby Regime
The forward-loaded peak ($a_1 = -0.55$) combined with the high $r_1$ created a very steep "forehead" on the airfoil. In a fluid simulation, this would cause a massive stagnation point and high pressure drag, likely resulting in a much lower reward than the "waisted" Design 1.

### Final Assessment
The "Thick-Convex" strategy effectively cured the geometric deformities (concavity and beaking), but it over-corrected. The resulting shape lacks the **fineness ratio** (length-to-thickness) necessary for flight. The next iteration should attempt to keep the convexity (moderate $r_1, r_3$) while significantly increasing the longitudinal stretch (maximizing $r_0, r_2$).
