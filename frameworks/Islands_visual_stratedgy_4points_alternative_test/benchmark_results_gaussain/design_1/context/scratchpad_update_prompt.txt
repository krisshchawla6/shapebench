# Airfoil Optimization Reference Card

## Global Heuristics
*   **Chord-Thickness Ratio:** Small horizontal distance between CP0 (LE) and CP2 (TE) relative to CP1/CP3 height results in "stubby/pebble" shapes with high pressure drag [iter 1].
*   **Flow Attachment:** Steep recovery slopes (high CP1 Radius + forward CP1 Angle) trigger massive flow separation [iter 1].
*   **Asymmetry Effects:** Extreme radius disparity between CP1 (Upper) and CP3 (Lower) produces "comma" or "hooked" profiles rather than flat bottoms [iter 1].
*   **Stretching:** Increasing X-axis distance between CP0 and CP2 is required to transition from "teardrop" to "aerodynamic" archetypes [iter 1].

### Visual Analysis of Outcome
The resulting shape is a **stubby, triangular wedge** with a concave posterior. It bears almost no resemblance to a "high-performance" or "stretched" airfoil. ### Synthesis of Design Failure

The primary failure stems from a **fundamental misunderstanding of the parameter mapping**, specifically regarding chord length (X-axis extension).

1.  **The "Stretch" Paradox:** The designer attempted to use `CP2 radius` to "pull" the airfoil into a long shape. In this coordinate system, the control points are likely distributed angularly. Increasing the radius of CP2 didn't move the point further away in X; it likely just made the "influence" of the rear control point much larger, bloating the back of the shape into a blunt wedge.
2.  **Scale Disparity:** Because the design never achieved a long chord, all other "fine-tuning" (like shifting max thickness or smoothing the nose) was applied to a "pebble" geometry. Aerodynamic features like "35% chord thickness" are meaningless if the total chord is nearly equal to the thickness.
3.  **The "Edgy" Backfire:** Setting `CP2 edgy` to 1.0 was intended to create a sharp point. However, when combined with a high radius (bloating) and no longitudinal stretch, it created a sharp *internal* corner or a flat base, effectively creating a "base drag" nightmare rather than a Kutta-compliant trailing edge.

### Key Learnings for the Parameter Space

*   **Radius $\neq$ Chord Length:** The "radius" parameter likely controls the local curvature or the weight of the spline at that sector, not the absolute Cartesian distance from the leading edge. To get a "stretched" airfoil, the designer needs to find which parameter (if any) controls the global aspect ratio, or realize that the points are constrained within a unit circle/box.
*   **The "Stubby" Default:** The system gravitates toward "blobs" and "wedges." To break out of this, one must likely *reduce* radii of the mid-sections (CP1, CP3) to thin the profile, rather than trying to *increase* the radius of the TE to stretch it.
*   **Leading Edge Sensitivity:** A negative "edgy" value at the nose (CP0) does not guarantee a blunt nose if the radius is too small; it just guarantees a mathematically smooth transition into a very sharp point.
*   **Concavity Trap:** High radius values on CP3 (lower surface) combined with specific angles can easily create concave "hooks" that trap flow and destroy lift. A "flat" lower surface likely requires a very specific, likely lower, radius value.
