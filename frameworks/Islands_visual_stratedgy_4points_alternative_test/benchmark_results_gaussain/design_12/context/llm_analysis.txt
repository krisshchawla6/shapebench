- **Geometry Observation**: 
    - The airfoil in the images (Design 1) is a very thick, "pebble-like" bluff body with a high thickness-to-chord ratio.
    - The leading edge is rounded, but the trailing edge is relatively blunt despite a sharp parameter setting, likely because the chord is too short to allow for a tapered closure.
    - There is significant curvature on both the upper and lower surfaces, making it look more like an ellipse than an aerodynamic wing.
- **Flow Field Observation**:
    - **Pressure**: There is a large stagnation point at the nose and a significant pressure drag due to the lack of pressure recovery at the rear.
    - **Velocity (U)**: A massive wake region is visible downstream, indicating early flow separation and high profile drag.
    - **Velocity (V)**: Large vertical displacements of the fluid show that the flow is struggling to remain attached to the steep slopes of the high-radius upper surface.
- **Mapping & Fixes**:
    - Design 1's high CP1 Radius (0.929) is the primary culprit for the "fat" geometry; it pushes the upper surface out nearly as far as the LE/TE points.
    - Design 2 achieved a positive reward (0.1886) by reducing the upper surface radius (0.528), though its chord was still quite short (CP2 r=0.128).
    - Key changes needed: (1) Maximize chord length by pushing CP0 and CP2 radii to the limit (>0.9), (2) Aggressively reduce side radii (CP1/CP3) to thin the profile, and (3) Move the CP1 peak forward to promote laminar flow.
