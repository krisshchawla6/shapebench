### Key Learnings and Design Evolution

**1. The "Radius = Distance" Realization**
The most critical takeaway from this result is that the `radius` parameter for the control points likely acts as a distance from a central origin. By reducing the Trailing Edge (CP2) radius from 0.677 (Design 1) to 0.145, you did not "taper" the tail; you **truncated the chord**. To achieve a streamlined airfoil, the LE and TE radii must be maximized to stretch the shape horizontally, while the Upper and Lower radii must be minimized to compress it vertically.

**2. The Scale Trap is Relative**
The airfoil in the image is significantly smaller than Design 1. In aerodynamic simulations (like CFD), a smaller chord length at the same flow velocity results in a lower Reynolds number and usually a much lower Lift-to-Drag ratio. The "Thimble" shape is essentially a bluff body with a sharp corner, which will still suffer from massive pressure drag because the flow has no surface to "re-attach" to or recover pressure along.

**3. Geometric Singularity vs. Taper**
You successfully used `Edgy = 1.0` to create a sharp point. However, a sharp point on a stubby object is just a "sharp wedge." For an airfoil to be effective, that sharp point must be the termination of a gradual convergence. 

**4. Strategic Pivot for Design 3**
To escape the "Pebble/Thimble" morphology:
*   **Increase CP0 and CP2 Radii:** Push the LE and TE as far apart as possible to maximize chord length.
*   **Decrease CP1 and CP3 Radii:** Keep the top and bottom surfaces close to the centerline to ensure a high fineness ratio (thinness).
*   **Maintain TE Edgy:** Keep the sharp point, but place it far away from the nose.
