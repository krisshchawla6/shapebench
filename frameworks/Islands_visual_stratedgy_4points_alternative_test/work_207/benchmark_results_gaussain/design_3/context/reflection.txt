### Key Learnings

1.  **The "Camber Trap":** Your attempt to create camber by drastically reducing the radius of CP6 (0.13) relative to CP2 (0.33) backfired. In this coordinate system, a very low radius doesn't just "flatten" the surface; it pulls the perimeter toward the center, creating a concave indentation that increases drag and destroys lift.
2.  **Spline Sensitivity (Lumpiness):** The "edginess" parameter ($e$) has a massive impact on the curvature continuity. By mixing high negative values (CP0) with high positive values (CP4) and neutral values (CP2/CP6) in close proximity, you created a "wavy" profile. Future iterations should aim for more gradual transitions in edginess.
3.  **Angular Offset Distortion:** Using significant angular offsets ($a \approx 0.15$ to $0.21$) on the mid-section points (CP2 and CP6) shifted the thickness distribution, but also contributed to the "pinched" look near the trailing edge. These offsets need to be used more conservatively to maintain a streamlined chord.
4.  **Scaling and Orientation:** The resulting shape is much smaller than the baseline circle and appears "tilted." This suggests that the interaction between the radial distances and the angular offsets is rotating the principal axis of the airfoil away from the horizontal flow.
5.  **Trailing Edge Geometry:** A sharp trailing edge (Kutta condition) requires not just high edginess at CP4, but also "support" from CP3 and CP5. If CP3 and CP5 have radii that are too large or angles that are too wide, they will "blunt" the effect of CP4's sharpness.
