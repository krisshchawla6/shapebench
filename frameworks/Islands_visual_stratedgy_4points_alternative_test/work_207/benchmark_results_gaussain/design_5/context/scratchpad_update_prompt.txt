### Key Learnings

#### 1. The "Dumbbell" Trap
By setting the polar points (CP0 and CP4) to near-maximum radius ($0.95-1.0$) while pulling the mid-section points (CP2, CP3, CP5, CP6) to low values ($0.24-0.38$), you created a "dumbbell" effect. The geometry engine struggles to maintain a convex airfoil shape when the "waist" of the wing is significantly thinner than the "head" and "tail."

#### 2. Edginess is Relative to Curvature
Your prediction that $e=1.0$ at CP4 would "force the flow to leave smoothly" failed because the local radius was too high. A high radius (1.0) spread the "sharpness" over such a large area that it manifested as a broad curve rather than a point. To get a true trailing edge, the radius usually needs to be balanced with the surrounding points to allow the edginess to "pinch" into a vertex.

#### 3. Concavity = Aerodynamic Failure
The resulting shape features significant concavity (the "neck"). In subsonic aerodynamics, concave surfaces (except near the trailing edge of specific supercritical airfoils) almost always trigger immediate boundary layer separation. This shape likely has higher drag than the baseline circle because the flow must navigate a "neck" and then hit a flat-faced bulb.

#### 4. Mapping Misalignment
The "beak" appeared on the left (CP0) and the "bulb" on the right (CP4). If this were an airfoil, it would be flying "backward" (sharp leading edge, blunt trailing edge). This indicates a need to swap the logic: the trailing edge (CP4) requires a specific combination of low radius and high edginess to "stretch" the tail out, while the leading edge (CP0) needs a moderate radius to remain blunt.

#### 5. The "Pinching" Limit
You noted a desire to keep radii $> 0.25$ to prevent separation. However, even at $0.32$, the transition from $r=1.0$ (at the poles) to $r=0.32$ (at the shoulders) was too steep. The "slope" of the radius change between adjacent control points is more critical for flow attachment than the absolute radius value.
