# Airfoil Optimization Reference Card

## General Design Rules
*   **Flow Direction**: Right-to-Left. CP0=LE (Nose); CP4=TE (Tail) [iter 2].
*   **TE Sharpness**: CP4 must be $r, e = 1.0$ to satisfy Kutta condition and reduce drag [iter 5].
*   **Radius Monotonicity**: Avoid "necking." Radii must transition smoothly; if $r_{CP4}=1.0$, then $r_{CP3/5}$ must be sufficiently high ($>0.6$) to prevent an "hourglass" spike [iter 8].
*   **Camber Limits**: Maintain $r_{CP2} > r_{CP6}$ for lift, but keep $\Delta r < 0.2$ to prevent lower surface collapse/concavity [iter 8].
*   **Concavity Risk**: Non-monotonic thickness (pinching at CP3/CP5) creates "dead air" pockets and flow separation [iter 5, 8].
*   **Scale Maintenance**: Avoid reducing all radii simultaneously; this shrinks the chord and creates a "bulbous" LE [iter 8].

## New Evidence (Iteration 8)
*   **The Necking Effect**: Setting CP4 to $r=1.0$ while neighbors (CP3/CP5) are $r \approx 0.48$ creates a non-monotonic "spike." This "hourglass" shape triggers massive flow separation [iter 8].
*   **Camber vs. Collapse**: A radius delta between CP2 and CP6 is necessary for lift, but $r_{CP6} = 0.32$ was too low, resulting in a "pinched" or concave underside rather than a smooth pressure surface [iter 8].
*   **Volume/Chord Loss**: Reducing all radii relative to the baseline shrinks the airfoil's effective size, making the LE appear disproportionately bulbous if not balanced [iter 8].
*   **Vertex Stability**: $e=1.0$ at CP4 successfully creates a sharp TE, but requires high radius support from CP3/CP5 to function as an airfoil rather than a needle [iter 8].
