This analysis critiques the **hybrid_stabilized_camber_v1** design based on the provided outcome image and the designer's stated goals.

### Parameter Comparison: Prediction vs. Outcome

| Parameter | Prediction | Outcome | Analysis |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **CP0 (Leading Edge)** | $r=0.9125, e=-0.4215$: Create a robust, rounded nose to prevent separation. | The "nose" (right side) is indeed rounded but appears disproportionately large compared to the rest of the body. | While the nose is rounded, the overall reduction in other radii has made the leading edge the most dominant feature, creating a "bulbous" front rather than a streamlined one. |
| **CP2 (Top)** | $r=0.5543$: Define upper thickness and prevent "tadpole" constriction. | The top surface is relatively smooth, but the transition to the trailing edge is very steep. | The radius was likely still too low relative to the chord length, contributing to the "tadpole" shape the designer explicitly sought to avoid. |
| **CP6 (Bottom)** | $r=0.3245$: Create positive camber by being lower than CP2. | There is extreme asymmetry, but instead of camber, it created a "pinched" underside. | The delta between CP2 (0.55) and CP6 (0.32) was too aggressive. | **CP4 (Trailing Edge)** | $r=1.0, e=1.0$: Create a perfectly sharp trailing edge to satisfy the Kutta condition. | A sharp point was successfully created, but it is pointing to the left (upstream if flow is L $\to$ R). | The geometry successfully reached a point, but the "sharpness" is coupled with a very narrow "neck," making it look like a tail rather than an airfoil TE. |
| **CP3 & CP5 (Transitions)** | $r \approx 0.48$: Provide a gradual taper to the TE and prevent separation. | These points failed to provide a gradual taper; instead, they created a "waist" or "necking" effect. | Even at 0.48, these radii were too small compared to the $r=1.0$ at CP4. This created a "bottleneck" where the shape thins out before expanding back to the TE point. |

### Key Learnings

1.  **The "Neck" Effect (Radius Ratios):**
    The most significant failure in this iteration was the ratio between the transition points (CP3, CP5) and the trailing edge (CP4). By setting CP4 to $r=1.0$ and the neighbors to $\approx 0.48$, the designer created a shape that pinches inward before flaring out to the point. This "hourglass" or "tadpole" geometry is aerodynamically disastrous as it triggers massive flow separation in the concave regions.

2.  **Camber vs. Distortion:**
    The "Decoupling Strategy" (setting CP6 significantly lower than CP2) was intended to create lift. However, in this parameter space, a radius of $0.32$ (CP6) is likely below the threshold for maintaining a convex hull when the neighboring points are also constricted. This resulted in a "collapsed" bottom surface rather than a smooth cambered one.

3.  **Orientation and Scale:**
    The resulting shape is much smaller in volume than the Baseline Circle (Design 1). While reducing thickness is generally good for airfoils, reducing *all* radii (except the TE) simultaneously causes the shape to shrink toward the origin. The sharp point (CP4) is on the left, suggesting the coordinate system or flow direction needs to be carefully re-verified to ensure the "Trailing Edge" isn't actually facing into the wind.

4.  **Edginess Sensitivity:**
    The use of $e=1.0$ at CP4 successfully created a vertex, but without sufficient radius support from CP3 and CP5, it creates a "spike." For a stable airfoil, the transition from the midsection to the TE must be monotonic (constantly decreasing in thickness) to keep the flow attached. This design is non-monotonic (it gets thinner, then thicker at the spike).
