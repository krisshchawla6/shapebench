This analysis evaluates the "Exploitation_Cambered_Stabilized_TE" design. While the strategy aimed to move from a circle to a high-performance airfoil, the resulting geometry is a distorted, non-aerodynamic "bulbous" shape that likely suffers from severe separation and low reward.

### Per-Parameter Analysis: Prediction vs. Outcome

| Parameter | Prediction | Outcome |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **CP0 (LE) [r: 0.86, e: -0.52]** | Blunt, rounded leading edge for smooth stagnation. | The LE is extremely blunt and appears nearly flat or slightly concave. The high radius relative to CP1/CP7, combined with negative edginess, created a "hammerhead" effect rather than a parabolic nose. |
| **CP1/CP7 [r: 0.66, e: -0.15]** | Smooth flow acceleration onto suction/pressure surfaces. | These points have significantly lower radii than the LE (CP0) and the rear-support (CP3/CP5). This created a "pinched" neck at the front of the airfoil, causing immediate flow separation. |
| **CP2 [r: 0.54, e: 0.05]** | Define maximum thickness point of the suction side. | At $r=0.54$, this is actually the *thinnest* part of the upper surface because CP3 is much larger ($r=0.76$). This created a concave "saddle" on the top of the airfoil. |
| **CP3/CP5 [r: 0.7655, e: 0.45]** | Anti-necking support to ensure a gradual transition to TE. | **Failure.** Instead of preventing a neck, these created a massive "bulb" at the rear. Because their radii are ~40% larger than CP2, the airfoil expands toward the back before hitting the TE. |
| **CP4 (TE) [r: 1.0, e: 1.0]** | Sharp vertex to satisfy the Kutta condition. | A sharp point is present, but it is attached to a bulbous rear section. The flow would likely separate long before reaching this point, rendering the Kutta condition moot. |
| **CP6 [r: 0.41, e: 0.0]** | Create camber necessary for lift. | While it is lower than CP2, the surrounding geometry (the bulb at CP5 and the pinch at CP7) creates a highly irregular lower surface that would produce massive drag rather than clean lift. |

### Key Learnings

**1. The "Anti-Necking" Radius Paradox**
The attempt to prevent "necking" (where the TE pinches in too fast) resulted in the opposite failure: a "bulbous" trailing edge. For a shape to be an airfoil, the radius must generally be monotonic (or nearly so) from the point of maximum thickness to the trailing edge. Setting $r_{CP3} > r_{CP2}$ is a fundamental geometric error that creates a "molar tooth" shape.

**2. Relative Radii Matter More than Absolute Values**
The strategy focused on "Optimal Ranges" for individual points but ignored the *gradient* between points. An airfoil is a continuous curve; if CP0 is $0.86$, CP1 is $0.66$, and CP2 is $0.54$, the shape is rapidly shrinking. If CP3 then jumps back to $0.76$, the geometry becomes wavy. Smoothness requires a logical progression of radii (e.g., $0.8 \rightarrow 0.7 \rightarrow 0.6 \rightarrow 0.5 \rightarrow 0.7 \rightarrow 1.0$).

**3. Negative Edginess and "Nose Collapse"**
The use of negative edginess at the leading edge (CP0) was intended to round the nose, but when combined with a radius that is much larger than the adjacent points (CP1/CP7), it pulls the surface inward between the control points, leading to the flattened/concave front profile seen in the result.

**4. Camber requires Global Coordination**
Camber isn't just about $r_{CP2} > r_{CP6}$; it requires the entire mean camber line to be curved. Because the rear of this design (CP3/CP5) is so thick and symmetric, any lift generated at the mid-section is likely negated by the massive, turbulent wake generated by the rear "bulb."
