### Critical Analysis of Design Outcomes

**1. The "Inverted Thickness" Problem:**
Standard airfoils are thickest near the 25-30% chord mark and taper toward the ends. Your design is **thickest at the ends** ($r_0=1.0, r_4=0.95$) and **thinnest in the middle** ($r_2, r_6 \approx 0.5$). This creates a "bluff-body-link-bluff-body" geometry. Aerodynamically, the flow will see the leading edge as a large obstacle, struggle to follow the "neck" inward, and then crash into the trailing edge bulb, causing massive turbulence.

**2. Edginess Miscalculation:**
*   **Trailing Edge:** To get a sharp TE, you need the angular offsets of $r_1$ and $r_7$ to move *away* from $r_0$, combined with a high negative edginess. Here, the radial support was too "crowded," resulting in a rounded protrusion.
*   **Leading Edge:** An edginess of $0.85$ on the nose effectively turns the spline into a square-ish block. For a "stagnation-minimizing" nose, a lower edginess (closer to 0.0 or slightly negative) is usually better to maintain a smooth circle-to-ellipse transition.

**3. Chord vs. Radius:**
You attempted to define a chord by setting $r_0$ and $r_4$ to 1.0. However, in a polar coordinate system, if the "sides" ($r_2, r_6$) are only 0.5, you haven't made a long airfoil; you've made a small object with two long spikes. The "effective chord" is very short because the thickness ratio is nearly 1:1 at the peaks.

### Key Learnings for the Next Iteration

*   **Reverse the Radial Profile:** To achieve a streamlined shape, the "middle" radii ($r_1, r_2, r_3$ and $r_7, r_6, r_5$) should generally be *larger* or equal to the chord ends, or the chord ends should be the primary extensions with the rest of the body tapering toward them.
*   **Smoothness over "Support":** Even if a radial delta of 0.3 is "legal," it is too aggressive for smooth flow. Aim for deltas of $<0.1$ between adjacent control points to maintain the laminar boundary layer.
*   **The "Pinch" Technique:** To get a sharp Trailing Edge ($r_0$), try reducing the radii of $r_1$ and $r_7$ significantly while keeping $r_0$ at 1.0. This "stretches" the tail out into a point.
*   **Camber vs. Symmetry:** The lumpy nature of the current design makes the 0.07 difference between $r_2$ and $r_6$ irrelevant. Focus on a smooth, global "tear-drop" shape before fine-tuning camber.
