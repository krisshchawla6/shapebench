## Objective
Optimize an airfoil shape to maximize aerodynamic performance (reward).

## Designer's Predictions
- **Hybrid Strategy Application**: I am hybridizing the geometric stability of the circle (uniformity and lack of "lumps") with the proportions of a low-speed NACA 4-series airfoil. I will keep the design relatively "thick" compared to high-performance wings to ensure the solver remains stable, while introducing clear functional features (LE, TE, Camber).
- **The Pinch for TE**: Following Iteration 6 rules, I will set $r_0$ to a high value (0.85) and use $r_1, r_7$ as bridge points to "stretch" the spline into a sharp point, using a negative $e_0$ to enforce a Kutta condition cusp.
- **Camber for Lift**: By setting $r_2 = 0.32$ and $r_6 = 0.28$, I create a subtle vertical asymmetry ($r_2 > r_6$). This should shift the stagnation point and generate a pressure differential between the surfaces.
- **Max Thickness Shift**: Using the `angle_param` ($a_2, a_6$), I will shift the max thickness points slightly forward toward the leading edge. This mimics successful natural and engineered airfoils where thickness peaks around 30% chord.
- **Laminar Delta Rule**: I have carefully tuned the radii to ensure that the difference between any two adjacent control points ($\Delta r$) does not exceed 0.35, which should prevent the "Radial Collapse" and "Peanut" penalties that ruined Design 5.
- **Optimization Goal**: This design moves the search from "bluff body" (circle) to "streamlined body" (teardrop). Even a modest improvement in drag reduction and lift generation will significantly advance the population reward beyond -0.0036.

## Per-Parameter Predictions

- **param[0][0] (r0) to 0.8500**: I predict this will CAUSE a significant extension of the trailing edge, increasing the chord length relative to the thickness.
- **param[0][2] (e0) to -0.8500**: I predict this will CAUSE a sharp, pinched trailing edge (Kutta cusp), encouraging flow to leave the surface tangentially and reducing pressure drag.
- **param[4][0] (r4) to 0.4800**: I predict this will CAUSE a defined leading edge that is significantly shorter than the trailing edge, moving the aerodynamic center forward.
- **param[4][2] (e4) to 0.3500**: I predict this will CAUSE a blunter, rounded nose profile, which improves stall characteristics and smooths the transition of the stagnation point.
- **param[2][0] (r2) to 0.3200 and param[6][0] (r6) to 0.2800**: I predict this asymmetry will CAUSE a cambered profile, generating positive lift.
- **param[2][1] (a2) to 0.1800 and param[6][1] (a6) to -0.1800**: I predict this will CAUSE the upper and lower "shoulders" of the airfoil to shift forward, placing the maximum thickness closer to the leading edge.

In the provided images of Design 1 (representative of the current population), I observe the following:

- **Geometry**:
    - The shape is a perfect unit circle (cylinder), exhibiting perfect bilateral and vertical symmetry.
    - All 8 control points are placed at an equal radial distance (radius_param = 1.0) with zero angular offset or edginess.
    - There is no distinct leading or trailing edge, and the thickness-to-chord ratio is 100%, which is extremely high for aerodynamic performance.
    - The surface is perfectly smooth with no discontinuities, but lacks any streamlined features.

- **Flow Field / Simulation Results**:
    - The pressure plot shows classic symmetric flow around a cylinder: high-pressure stagnation points at the front and back (yellow/green) and low-pressure zones on the top and bottom (dark purple).
    - The velocity/wake plot shows a massive, symmetric separated wake extending far downstream, indicating very high pressure drag.
    - There is no lift generated (zero asymmetry in pressure), and the reward of -0.0036 reflects the high drag coefficient of a bluff body.
    - No visible vortex shedding is captured in this steady-state snapshot, but the wide wake confirms the flow is not remaining attached.

Design 1's action vector `[1.0, 0.0, 0.0, ...]` directly maps to this circular geometry. The primary geometric flaws are the lack of a sharp trailing edge (causing wake separation) and the excessive thickness. To improve the population, we must:
1.  **Reduce thickness**: Lower the radial values for the top ($r_2$) and bottom ($r_6$) points.
2.  **Introduce a Trailing Edge**: Greatly extend $r_0$ while "pinching" $r_1$ and $r_7$ to create a teardrop taper.
3.  **Break Symmetry**: Implement camber by making the upper surface ($r_2$) larger than the lower surface ($r_6$).
