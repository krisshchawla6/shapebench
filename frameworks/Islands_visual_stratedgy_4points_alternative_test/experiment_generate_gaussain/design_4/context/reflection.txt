### Key Learnings

1.  **The "Radial Collapse" Trap**: You attempted to create a slender airfoil by making the "height" parameters ($r_2, r_6$) small while keeping the "length" parameters ($r_0, r_4$) large. However, if the intermediate points ($r_1, r_7$, etc.) are not carefully graduated, the spline collapses inward, creating a **concave bean shape** instead of a convex airfoil.
2.  **Concavity is an Aerodynamic Killer**: The deep notch on the lower surface will cause immediate flow separation and a massive pressure drag penalty. Real airfoils are almost exclusively convex (or very slightly concave near the trailing edge only).
3.  **Eccentricity ($e$) is Context-Dependent**: A high negative $e$ does not guaranteed a sharp point if the surrounding geometry is "pulling" the spline in a different direction. To get a sharp trailing edge, the radii of the points immediately adjacent to the tail ($r_1$ and $r_7$) must also be relatively large and taper gradually toward $r_0$.
4.  **Symmetry vs. Performance**: Your move toward asymmetry ($r_2 \neq r_6$) is correct for generating lift, but the ratio was too extreme. An airfoil with a thickness-to-chord ratio of roughly 12-15% is usually ideal; here, the "waist" of the airfoil is too thin compared to the "head" and "tail."

**Verdict**: The "Cambered_Slender_Explorer" failed because it prioritized extreme boundary values over **curvature continuity**. The resulting shape is a bluff body with a high-drag concave feature, likely resulting in a reward similar to or worse than the baseline circle.
