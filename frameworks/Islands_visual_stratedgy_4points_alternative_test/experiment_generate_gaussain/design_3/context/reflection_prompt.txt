## Objective
Optimize an airfoil shape to maximize aerodynamic performance (reward).

## Designer's Predictions
*   **Strategy Focus (Exploration):** The current population is clustered at the "circle" origin. I am moving to an underexplored boundary by pushing $e_0$ and $e_4$ to their respective limits (-1.0 and 1.0) and utilizing $r$ values that create an extremely thin, high-camber profile.
*   **Chord Maximization:** By setting $r_0$ near 1.0 and $r_4$ near 0.85, I am exploring a high-aspect-ratio design space that the current "circle-heavy" population lacks.
*   **Thin-Film Aerodynamics:** I am testing the lower limits of the $r$ parameter ($r_6 \approx 0.08$) to see if the optimizer handles near-zero thickness. This pushes the geometry toward a "cambered plate" style, which is a significant departure from the current thick bluff body.
*   **Angular Biasing:** I am introducing non-zero $a$ values (angle offsets) for the first time in the population. Shifting $a_2$ (upper surface) aft and $a_6$ (lower surface) forward should create a more efficient pressure distribution by delaying the suction peak.
*   **Sharp vs. Blunt Polarity:** Following the Reference Card, I am applying extreme polarity to the edges ($e_0 = -0.99$, $e_4 = 0.85$) to definitively test the effect of edge geometry on the wake size.

## Per-Parameter Predictions

*   **r[0][0] to 0.9850**: I predict this will extend the trailing edge to the maximum possible distance from the origin, defining a long chord.
*   **r[0][2] to -0.9950**: I predict this will sharpen the trailing edge into a needle-like point, forcing the flow to leave smoothly and reducing the wake.
*   **r[4][0] to 0.8500**: I predict this will create a distinct forward protrusion for the leading edge.
*   **r[4][2] to 0.8800**: I predict this will broaden the curvature at the nose, ensuring the stagnation point doesn't trigger separation at the front.
*   **r[2][0] to 0.2800**: I predict this will significantly "squash" the circle's height, creating a streamlined subsonic profile.
*   **r[6][0] to 0.0850**: I predict this will pull the lower surface very close to the center, creating a high-camber, thin-profile geometry.
*   **r[1,3,5,7][0] (Intermediate values)**: I am setting these to roughly 0.45-0.55 to bridge the gap between the long chord and thin height, preventing the "kinks" mentioned in the Iteration 3 Reference Card.

The images provided show **Design 1**, which currently exists as a perfect circle. 

**Geometry Observations:**
*   The shape is a perfectly symmetric, smooth unit circle defined by eight equidistant control points.
*   There is no distinct leading or trailing edge, resulting in a shape that lacks any aerodynamic orientation.
*   The thickness-to-chord ratio is 1.0 (100%), which is extremely high for subsonic flow and typical of a bluff body rather than a streamlined airfoil.
*   The geometry is entirely convex but lacks the tapering necessary to manage flow recovery at the rear.

**Flow Field / Simulation Observations:**
*   **Stagnation and Pressure:** A prominent stagnation point is visible at the leading face (9 o'clock position), indicated by the high-pressure/low-velocity region.
*   **Flow Separation:** The flow separates almost immediately after passing the vertical centerline (12 and 6 o'clock positions), as the adverse pressure gradient is too steep for the boundary layer to remain attached.
*   **Wake:** A massive, symmetric wake of low-velocity, recirculating flow (deep purple in velocity plots) extends far behind the cylinder. This indicates extremely high pressure drag.
*   **Lift:** Because the flow is perfectly symmetric top-to-bottom, the net lift is zero (reward -0.0036 is likely a small numerical error or purely drag-dominated).

**Design 1 Mapping & Needed Changes:**
*   **Action Mapping:** The vector `[1.0, 0.0, 0.0, ...]` across all points results in a circle because all radii are identical and edgy parameters are neutral.
*   **Key Changes Needed:**
    1.  **Chord Definition:** Drastically increase the ratio of horizontal length ($r_0, r_4$) to vertical thickness ($r_2, r_6$).
    2.  **Kutta Condition:** Force $e_0$ toward -1.0 to create a sharp trailing edge, which is essential for establishing circulation and lift.
    3.  **Leading Edge Profiling:** Use a positive $e_4$ to maintain a rounded nose that can handle varying angles of attack.
    4.  **Camber Introduction:** Reduce $r_6$ (lower surface) significantly more than $r_2$ (upper surface) to create the asymmetry required for lift.
