This analysis examines the transition from the "collapsed" geometry of Design 1 to the "stubby" geometry of Design 2 (Cambered_Convex_V1).

### Key Learnings

#### 1. The "Goldilocks Zone" for Thickness
In Design 1, radii of ~0.20 produced a collapsed, non-physical line. In Design 2, radii of ~0.60–0.85 produced a "pebble" or "bean" shape. 
*   **The Problem**: An airfoil with a chord length of ~2.0 (from LE at $r=1, a=0$ to TE at $r=1, a=\pi$) and mid-body radii of ~0.7 creates a thickness-to-chord ratio of roughly 70%. 
*   **The Fix**: To achieve a standard aerodynamic thickness (10-15%), the mid-body radii ($r_1, r_2, r_4, r_5$) likely need to be significantly lower, perhaps in the **0.15 to 0.40** range, provided the `edgy_param` remains negative to prevent collapse.

#### 2. Polar Coordinate Sensitivity
The designer successfully used asymmetry ($r_{upper} > r_{lower}$) to generate camber. However, in a polar system, as the points move toward the LE ($a=0$), the vertical distance between an upper point and a lower point diminishes rapidly. To maintain thickness and camber near the nose, the angular spacing ($a$) needs to be carefully tuned alongside the radii.

#### 3. Successful "Healing" of the Geometry
The most significant improvement is the use of `edgy_param = -1.0`. This successfully "healed" the geometry from the jagged mess of Design 1 into a continuous, differentiable manifold. This is a prerequisite for any valid CFD solution, even if the current shape is too "draggy" (bluff body) to produce high reward.

#### 4. The "Stinger" Effect
Setting $e=1.0$ at the trailing edge while the rest of the body is $e=-1.0$ creates a very specific geometric feature: a smooth bulbous body that terminates in a sharp needle. While aerodynamically better than a blunt rear, the transition from a very thick body to a sharp point causes massive flow separation. The body must become thinner overall for the Kutta condition (the sharp TE) to function effectively.
