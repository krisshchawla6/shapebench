## Reference Card: Airfoil Degree of Freedom (DoF) Mapping (Updated Iter 6)

### Global Design Rules
*   **Aspect Ratio & Chord**: Maximized by placing the LE (CP3) at $a \approx 0.0, r \approx 1.0$ and the TE (CP0/7) at the polar limits $a \approx \pm 1.0, r \approx 1.0$. Spanning the origin creates necessary volume [iter 3, 4, 5, 6].
*   **Structural Priority**: `angle_param` (chord) > `radius_param` (thickness/aspect ratio) > `edgy_param` (flow regulation/smoothing) [iter 1, 2, 4, 6].
*   **The "Goldilocks" Radius Zone**: 
    *   **Lower Bound**: $r < 0.3$ for intermediate points causes **radial collapse** (wasp-waist) or "shards" (jagged cut-backs) [iter 4, 6].
    *   **Upper Bound**: $r > 0.8$ for all points causes **chord compression** (slug/pebble shape) [iter 5].
    *   **Optimal**: LE/TE at $r \approx 1.0$, mid-body points at $r \approx 0.4–0.6$ to "stretch" the chord without creating a fragile "shard" [iter 5, 6].
*   **Kutta Condition**: A geometric singularity (high `edgy_param` $\approx 1.0$) at the Trailing Edge (CP0/7) is mandatory to define flow reattachment [iter 2, 3, 4, 5, 6].

---

### DoF 1: `radius_param` (Distance from Centroid)
*   **Chord Stretching**: High values (~0.9+) are required at the LE and TE poles to establish primary length [iter 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6].
*   **The Shard Effect**: Extreme radial gradients between the LE pole ($r \approx 1.0$) and immediate neighbors ($r < 0.3$) create a triangular "beak" rather than a rounded nose. Mid-body points must be $> 0.4$ to maintain a streamlined profile [iter 6].
*   **Camber & Lift**: Asymmetric radii between upper (CP4-6) and lower (CP1-2) surfaces create camber. A higher upper radius (e.g., $r=0.4$ vs $r=0.2$) creates a high-speed wedge/cambered profile [iter 4, 5, 6].

### DoF 2: `angle_param` (Angular Position)
*   **Polar Wrap-around**: $a=-1.0$ and $a=1.0$ are the same point (TE). The LE must be at $a \approx 0.0$ to maximize chord length [iter 3, 4, 5, 6].
*   **Parabolic Entry (Clustering)**: To avoid a "flat face" or "beak," neighboring angles must cluster tightly near the LE pole (e.g., $a \approx \pm 0.05$). Wide spreads ($a \approx \pm 0.25$) produce blunt or triangular leading edges [iter 5, 6].
*   **Monotonic Sequencing**: Angles must progress smoothly (TE $\to$ LE $\to$ TE). Non-monotonic jumps create "hooks" or self-intersecting loops [iter 2, 5, 6].

### DoF 3: `edgy_param` (Local Curvature/Sharpness)
*   **TE Flow Regulation**: High positive values ($e \approx 1.0$) at the Trailing Edge create the "stinger" necessary for flow separation [iter 2, 3, 4, 5, 6].
*   **Surface Healing**: Extreme negative values ($e \approx -1.0$) at mid-body points and the LE are the most effective way to "heal" a polygon into a smooth, continuous aerodynamic silhouette, eliminating "blockiness" [iter 1, 4, 5, 6].
*   **Leading Edge Nose**: $e \approx -1.0$ at the LE ensures smooth flow acceleration; however, it cannot override poor $r$ or $a$ values—if $r$ is too high, the nose stays flat; if $r$ transitions are too steep, the nose becomes a "beak" [iter 5, 6].

### Key Learnings

**1. The "Volume Trap": Over-correction leads to Bloat**
In an attempt to avoid the "pinched" mid-body of previous designs, the minimum radius was set too high ($>0.45$). For a shape to look like an airfoil, the "sides" (mid-body points) must have significantly lower radii than the "ends" (LE and TE). By keeping all radii high, the design became a square rather than a wing.

**2. Relative Radius is King**
An airfoil's performance comes from its **fineness ratio** (length vs. thickness). By pushing all $r$ values toward the outer boundary, the "thickness" became nearly 80% of the "length." High-performance airfoils typically have a thickness-to-chord ratio of 6% to 15%. This design is closer to 80%.

**3. LE Clustering requires Radius Gradient**
Clustering points at $a = \pm 0.08$ only creates a "nose" if those points have a lower radius than the LE pole (CP3). Because CP2 and CP4 had radii nearly as large as CP3, the "nose" flattened out into a blunt face, which would create massive drag (a "bluff body" effect).

**4. The "Stinger" requires Taper**
A sharp Trailing Edge ($e=1.0$) cannot create a Kutta condition if the preceding points (CP1 and CP6) do not "ramp down" toward it. The transition from $r=0.72$ (CP6) to the TE is too abrupt, creating a shape that looks more like a speech bubble than a wing.

**5. Strategy for Next Iteration**
To achieve the "High-Camber" goal without the "Blob" outcome, the mid-body radii (CP1, CP2, CP5, CP6) must be aggressively reduced (likely to the 0.1–0.3 range) while keeping the LE/TE radii high to maintain chord length. This will "stretch" the blob into a thin, functional foil.
