## Reference Card: Airfoil Degree of Freedom (DoF) Mapping

### Global Design Rules
*   **Aspect Ratio & Chord**: Aspect ratio is driven by `angle_param` distribution. A wide spread (e.g., -0.95 to 0.92) is required to pull points into a distinct horizontal chord [iter 1, 2]. 
*   **Structural Priority**: `angle_param` (chord/placement) > `radius_param` (thickness/scale) > `edgy_param` (flow regulation/smoothing) [iter 1, 2].
*   **The "Slug" vs. "Blob"**: While wide angles prevent a "blob," intermediate points must have significantly lower `radius_param` than the LE/TE to prevent a thick "slug" profile. High thickness-to-chord ($t/c$) ratios increase drag [iter 2].
*   **Kutta Condition Necessity**: A geometric singularity (high `edgy_param`) at the Trailing Edge is mandatory to define flow reattachment and move beyond zero reward [iter 2].

---

### DoF 1: `radius_param` (Distance from Centroid)
*   **Chord Stretching**: High values (~0.9+) at polar opposite angles are required to establish the Leading Edge (LE) and Trailing Edge (TE) [iter 1, 2].
*   **Thickness Control ($t/c$)**: Intermediate radii (points between LE and TE) must be significantly reduced (e.g., < 0.4) to thin the airfoil. High intermediate radii result in "slug" or "comma" shapes [iter 2].
*   **Asymmetric Camber**: Varying radii between the upper (points 1-3) and lower (points 5-7) surfaces creates thickness gradients, but requires coordinated angular placement to arch the mean camber line [iter 2].

### DoF 2: `angle_param` (Angular Position)
*   **Chord Establishment**: The most critical parameter for aspect ratio. Using extreme values (e.g., -0.95 and 0.92) successfully stretches the geometry into a longitudinal form [iter 1, 2].
*   **Clustering Failure**: Restricting values to a narrow range (e.g., +/- 0.2) bunches points into a non-aerodynamic "river stone" or "blob" [iter 1].
*   **Monotonic Sequencing**: Angles must progress smoothly from TE, around LE, and back to TE; non-monotonic jumps create geometric "kinks" or "hooks" that disrupt flow [iter 2].

### DoF 3: `edgy_param` (Local Curvature/Sharpness)
*   **Flow Regulation (TE)**: High positive values (e.g., 0.85) at the Trailing Edge create the "stinger" necessary for flow separation, fixing the 0.0 reward failure seen in rounded geometries [iter 2].
*   **Nose Rounding (LE)**: Negative values (e.g., -0.45 to -0.85) at the Leading Edge and suction side ensure smooth flow acceleration and prevent "lumpy" pressure distributions [iter 1, 2].
*   **Geometry Healing**: Extreme negative values (-0.95) force the smoothest possible transition between points, effectively "healing" polygons into continuous curves [iter 1].

### Key Learnings

1.  **Polar Coordinate Mapping Awareness**: The most critical failure was the assumption that $a=-1$ and $a=1$ are opposite ends of a line. In this parameter space, the angles likely represent a full circle ($-\pi$ to $\pi$). To create a horizontal airfoil, the LE and TE should be roughly $1.0$ unit apart in the action space (e.g., $a=0.0$ for the front and $a=1.0/-1.0$ for the back).
2.  **The "Origin" Trap**: When all intermediate radii are set very low, the shape is "sucked" into the center. If the LE and TE are not properly placed at opposite angles, the entire shape becomes a small object near the origin rather than a long body spanning the domain.
3.  **Sharpness vs. Curvature**: The `edgy_param` is highly effective. Even in this collapsed geometry, the distinction between the "needle" point (high $e$) and the "circular" point (low $e$) is visually obvious.
4.  **Scaling and Reward**: The reward remains near zero because the resulting shape is too small to generate significant lift and is oriented poorly relative to the flow. Aerodynamic performance requires the chord length to be maximized relative to thickness, which requires spreading the control points across the angular spectrum ($a$).

### Next Step Recommendation
To achieve the "blade" geometry, keep the $r$ and $e$ strategies but redistribute the angles ($a$). Set the TE points at $a \approx \pm 1.0$ and the LE point at $a \approx 0.0$. This will force the spline to stretch across the origin, creating the desired horizontal chord.
