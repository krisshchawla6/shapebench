## Objective
Optimize an airfoil shape to maximize aerodynamic performance (reward).

## Designer's Predictions
- **Apply Visual Intuition Strategy**: My primary goal is to "heal" the mangled geometry seen in Design 1. By visually smoothing the contour and adding volume, I aim to eliminate the massive separation bubble seen in the U-velocity field.
- **Geometric Regularization**: I am moving away from the "pinched" tail of Design 1 by keeping point 5 and 6's radii positive. This creates a "fuller" belly for the airfoil, which should stabilize the lower-surface pressure.
- **Leading Edge Robustness**: I will expand the radius at the nose (Point 0) and use a very smooth `edgy_param` to create a rounded leading edge, allowing the stagnation point to shift without triggering a massive suction peak.
- **Wake Reduction**: By creating a monotonic taper toward the trailing edge (Points 3 and 4), I predict the flow will remain attached longer, significantly reducing the wake size seen in the velocity plots.
- **Evolutionary Step**: This design moves the population from "randomly generated shapes" toward "engineered profiles" by enforcing thickness and smoothness, which are the two most glaring deficiencies in the current best design.

- **Point 0 [0.7250, 0.0000, -0.9500]**: Setting r0 to 0.725 and e0 to -0.95 to create a rounded, blunt nose for better angle-of-attack tolerance.

- **Geometry**: 
  - The design is pathologically thin, resembling a crumpled ribbon more than a functional aerodynamic surface.
  - There is a severe "pinch point" or self-intersection near the 75% chord mark on the lower surface where the geometry collapses inward.
  - The leading edge is excessively sharp, which typically leads to leading-edge stall at very low angles of attack.
  - The overall curvature is non-monotonic, creating multiple small "bumps" that disrupt smooth flow.
- **Flow Field / Simulation Results**: 
  - The velocity-U plot shows a massive, thick wake originating from the upper surface near the mid-chord, indicating total flow separation (stall).
  - The pressure plot shows a localized, extreme suction spike at the leading edge, but the pressure recovery is chaotic due to the geometric kinks.
  - The velocity-V plot confirms significant flow deflection only at the very front, with stagnant, recirculating flow over the majority of the aft section.
  - The reward of 1.0360 is likely dominated by a small amount of lift from the high-camber front section, but crippled by the high drag of the separated wake.

**Critical Assessment and Key Changes**:
- **Point 5 Flaw**: The `radius_param` of -1.0 in Design 1 at point 5 caused a catastrophic inward collapse. 
- **Sharpness Flaw**: High `edgy_param` values (like 1.0 at point 4) created sharp corners that triggered early separation.
- **Thickness Change**: Drastically increase the `radius_param` values for the upper surface to create a thicker, more conventional "tear-drop" profile.
- **Smoothing Change**: Set nearly all `edgy_param` values to be strongly negative to ensure a smooth, continuous curvature that the flow can follow.
- **Camber Normalization**: Avoid extreme negative radius values; instead, use moderate differences between upper and lower points to create balanced camber.
